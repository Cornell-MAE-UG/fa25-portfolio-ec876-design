## Overview
As part of MAE 3780, my team designed and built an autonomous robot to compete in a head-to-head block-clearing competition. The robot was tasked with navigating a colored arena, detecting boundaries via a color sensor, and corralling cubes within its perimeter using a passive funnel design. The project integrated mechanical design, electrical wiring, and bare-metal AVR C firmware development on an Arduino-based platform.
 
---
 
## Results
 
### 1. Robot Design Overview
The robot uses a passive collection strategy built around three core subsystems:
 
- **Mechanical:** A front funnel and side walls contain blocks within the robot's perimeter as it sweeps the arena. A front wall positioned near the wheels prevents the robot from flipping when blocks slide underneath.
- **Electrical:** A TCS3200 color sensor wired to an Arduino, with an H-bridge driving the left and right drive motors.
- **Software:** Bare-metal AVR C using direct port manipulation. The robot drives straight until the color sensor detects a boundary (color change or black line), then executes a backup-and-turn-right routine.

### 2. Mechanical Design
The final chassis used a simple, reliable cardboard funnel after a 3D-printed barrier prototype proved to be positioned too far forward, allowing blocks to collect outside the perimeter. Key design decisions:
 
- Side walls angled outward to funnel blocks toward the chassis center
- Front wall low enough to prevent under-sliding cubes from tipping the robot
- Passive collection — no actuators, no failure modes during a match
The team prioritized reliability over complexity, accepting reduced collection capacity in exchange for a subsystem with no risk of mechanical failure during competition.
 
---
 
### 3. Electrical System
The electrical system was built around an Arduino driving two DC motors through an H-bridge, with a TCS3200 color sensor providing boundary detection.
 
**Pin assignments:**
- Left motor: PB0 (forward), PB1 (backward)
- Right motor: PD4 (forward), PD5 (backward)
- Color sensor output: PB2 (D10)
**Color sensor calibration thresholds (Timer1 pulse counts):**
- Black: count < 20
- Blue: 20 ≤ count ≤ 100
- Yellow: count > 100
A 3-sample confirmation count was used to reject noisy single-frame misreads before triggering a turn.
 
---
 
### 4. Software Architecture
The firmware was written in bare-metal AVR C using direct port manipulation rather than the Arduino HAL, giving deterministic timing for the color sensor's frequency-output interface.
 
**Core control loop:**
1. Read starting color at boot (skip black readings during init)
2. Drive forward continuously
3. Sample color sensor each loop iteration
4. If color reads black, or if color flips between yellow and blue, increment confirmation counter
5. After 3 confirmed boundary samples: stop → back up 300 ms → turn right 500 ms → blind-drive forward 600 ms → resume sensing
**Key implementation details:**
- Timer1 configured with prescaler `(CS12 | CS10)` for pulse counting from TCS3200
- 156-tick measurement window per color sample
- All motor transitions include a 300 ms `SWITCH_PAUSE` to prevent H-bridge shoot-through

### 6. Competition Performance
The robot competed in seven round-robin matches, finishing with **3 wins** before being eliminated prior to the single-elimination bracket.
 
**Strengths:**
- The passive funnel was the most reliable subsystem in the entire competition — with no actuators, there was no risk of in-match failure. Any cube contacted at the right angle ended up inside the perimeter.
- The robot never failed to start and never got stuck on the arena border across all seven matches.
**Weaknesses:**
- Wheels were the primary failure mode. On contact with opposing robots, the wheels would dislodge from the motor shafts, severely diminishing mobility for the remainder of the match.
- The robot exhibited a persistent leftward pull despite equal motor voltage (verified with a multimeter) and no observable rubbing or slipping.
---
 
### 7. Design Process Reflection
The team progressed steadily through weekly milestones, but two issues persisted:
 
- **Leftward drift:** Never fully diagnosed despite electrical verification and mechanical inspection. Rubber bands over the wheels were attempted as a friction-balancing fix.
- **Arm/barrier design:** The 3D-printed barrier was geometrically incorrect — too far forward, causing collection failures. Time constraints forced a pivot to cardboard at the final milestone, sacrificing rigidity for delivery.
---
 
### 8. Conclusions and Lessons Learned
If repeated under the same constraints:
 
- **Start arm design earlier** to allow iteration on a sturdier structure than cardboard
- **Mechanically secure the wheels** to the motor shafts (set screws or threaded fasteners) rather than relying on press-fit
- **Prioritize reaching the arena center quickly** — the teams with the fastest dash to the middle collected disproportionately more blocks
- **Servos significantly increased win rates** for teams that used them — a worthwhile investment for active collection
For future students: simple and reliable beats complex and fragile. A working basic robot will outperform an ambitious design that fails mid-match.

## Summary
This project integrated mechanical prototyping, register-level embedded firmware, and competition strategy to produce a functional autonomous robot. While the final result fell short of the elimination bracket, the passive funnel and reliable boundary-detection logic demonstrated that disciplined simplicity in subsystem design produces consistent in-match behavior — a lesson directly applicable to future mechatronics projects.
