## Overview
My team designed and built an autonomous robot for the MAE 3780 block-clearing competition. It crossed the colored arena, detected boundaries with a color sensor, and collected cubes using a passive funnel. The project combined mechanical design, electrical wiring, and C/C++ coding on Arduino.
 
---
 
## Results
 
### 1. Robot Design Overview
The robot uses a passive collection strategy built around three core subsystems:
 
- **Mechanical:** The front funnel and side walls contain blocks as the robot sweeps the arena. A wall near the wheels stops flipping when blocks slide underneath.
- **Electrical:** A TCS3200 color sensor connects to Arduino. An H-bridge powers the drive motors.
- **Software:** The robot drives straight until the color sensor detects a boundary. It then backs up and turns right.

### 2. Mechanical Design
The chassis used a cardboard funnel after a 3D-printed barrier failed; it was too far forward, leaving blocks outside. Key design decisions:
 
- Side walls angled outward to funnel blocks to the center.
- The front wall is low enough to block under-sliding cubes from tipping the robot.
- Passive collection — no actuators, no failure modes during a match.
The team chose reliability over complexity, accepting less capacity for zero mechanical failure risk.
 
---
 
### 3. Electrical System
An Arduino drives two DC motors via an H-bridge. The TCS3200 sensor detects boundaries.
 
**Pin assignments:**
 
- Left motor: PB0 (forward), PB1 (backward)
- Right motor: PD4 (forward), PD5 (backward)
- Color sensor output: PB2 (D10)
**Color sensor calibration thresholds (Timer1 pulse counts):**
 
- Black: count < 20
- Blue: 20 ≤ count ≤ 100
- Yellow: count > 100
Three samples confirm a reading before a turn to reject noise.

### 5. Competition Performance
The robot played seven matches, won three, and was eliminated before the brackets.
 
**Strengths:**
 
- The passive funnel was reliable. No actuators meant no failures. Any cube hit at the right angle ended up inside.
- The robot consistently started and avoided getting stuck on the arena border throughout seven matches.
**Weaknesses:**
 
- Wheel failure was common. Opponent contact dislodged wheels, reducing mobility.
- The robot pulled left, though the motor voltages were equal, and no rubbing was observed.
---
 
### 6. Design Process Reflection
The team met milestones, but two issues persisted: leftward drift and arm design.
 
- **Leftward drift:** Never fully diagnosed, despite electrical and mechanical checks. Rubber bands tried to balance the friction.
- **Arm/barrier design:** The 3D-printed barrier was too far forward and failed. Time constraints led to a cardboard switch, sacrificing rigidity for delivery.
---
 
### 7. Conclusions and Lessons Learned
If repeated under the same constraints:
 
- Start arm design early to iterate on sturdier structures.
- Secure wheels mechanically to motor shafts with fasteners, not press-fit.
- Reach the arena center fast — teams that did so collected more blocks.
- Servos led to more wins — a good investment for active collection.
Simple and reliable beats complex and fragile. A basic working robot wins over ambitious failures.
 
---
 
## Summary
This project integrated prototyping, embedded firmware, and strategy to build an autonomous robot. Though the result missed brackets, the passive funnel and boundary logic showed how simplicity in subsystem design leads to consistent competition performance.
